    Success Story

    The Dundalk Institute of Technology (DkIT) Wave Activated Sensor Platform (WASP) project aims to deliver a self-powering, low-cost sensor platform which may be used for various sensing and communication applications in the marine environment. The concept draws on multiple renewable energy sources, including solar and wave power.

    The prototype device will be tested at the SmartBay test site off Irelandâ€™s west coast for 6 months, gathering data in a wide range of weather conditions. The Ocean DEMO programme funded the research and development activities of both the mechanical and electrical aspects of a wave energy power take-off system for the WASP.

    Using rapid prototyping techniques, Ocean DEMO allowed DkIT to create a custom turbine for the WASP. Testing procedures have been developed to design airflow management and select suitable generator options, all will help the WASP progress up to, and beyond, TRL 7. Thanks to the involvement of the student body at DkIT, Ocean DEMO is also inspiring the next generation of offshore marine renewable engineers.

    Next steps

    DkIT plan to continue developing the sensing and communication capabilities of the WASP platform, while optimising power generation and management. Future deployments for further benchmarking, device calibration, and optimisation at the SmartBay test site are planned for the near future.

    Multi-device plans

    The low-cost, self-powering design philosophy of the WASP will allow developers and local authorities to deploy multiple devices at a site, for extended uninterrupted periods of time. This will allow for both temporal and spatial resolution of multiple parameters, including significant wave height and zero cross period, across the site.
